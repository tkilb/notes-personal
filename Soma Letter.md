Dear Vanessa and Soma Leadership Team,

I’m writing as a member who has been part of this community for a little over three years. Soma has meant a lot to me. It’s where I deepened my practice, found teachers I trust, and felt connected to something grounded and intentional. I truly want to see Soma succeed and grow — which is why I feel compelled to share some concerns.

Lately, it feels like the studio is drifting away from what originally made it special. There seems to be a growing focus on HIIT-style formats, hot yoga trends, and pilates-inspired offerings. While I understand the need to evolve and stay competitive, I worry that in chasing what’s currently popular, Soma may be losing sight of what Yoga actually is — and what drew me here in the first place.

Yoga, especially traditional Vinyasa, is more than just a workout. It’s a practice rooted in breath, mindfulness, philosophy, and intentional movement. The lack of traditional Vinyasa (Yoga 2) classes has been especially disappointing. That was the heart of my practice at Soma, and it feels like it’s disappearing.

I’m also concerned about the increasing emphasis on super-heated classes. While heated rooms can feel intense and trendy, research has raised questions about the health benefits of extreme heat. Some studies suggest that excessive heat can increase cardiovascular strain, risk of dehydration, and dizziness, particularly in longer sessions. It’s not clear that hotter automatically means better — and I worry that we may be prioritizing intensity over sustainability and safety.

Additionally, I’ve noticed what feels like a push toward a more performance-driven, high-energy aesthetic — loud music, “blast”-style formats, and a vibe that sometimes resembles what other studios (PYB) are doing to stand out. I understand the desire to attract new members, but trying to replicate high-energy power yoga trends — complete with club-like music and hype — doesn’t necessarily align with the core values of yoga. Soma doesn’t need to be anyone else. It built its community by being grounded, thoughtful, and authentic.

Another concern is instructor autonomy. Soma has incredibly talented teachers, each with unique training and passions. When instructors are constrained to fit a specific branded format, it limits their ability to teach from their strengths. That authenticity is something members feel — and it’s part of what makes a studio truly special.

Please know this comes from a place of care. I want Soma to thrive, not just financially, but in integrity and purpose. I believe there’s a way to balance growth with staying rooted in what Yoga actually represents. I would love to see more traditional Vinyasa offerings, more room for instructor individuality, and a thoughtful reconsideration of how far we lean into fitness trends that may not reflect Soma’s original mission.

Thank you for taking the time to read this. I hope it’s received in the spirit it’s intended — as feedback from someone who truly values this community and wants to see it flourish in a way that feels aligned and sustainable.

Sincerely,

-Tyler
